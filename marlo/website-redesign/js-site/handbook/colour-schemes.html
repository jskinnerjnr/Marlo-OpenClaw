Colour Focus: Colour Schemes

Complement, Contrast or Connect — the three approaches to building the colour scheme for your classic car interior restoration.

← Back to Handbook

Whether you're looking to keep it subtle, make a colour statement or strike a contrast, there's a number of ways to use colour depending on the look and feel of your restoration. Here we explore all three approaches.

Complement

Complementary colour schemes use tonal colour across all areas of the car for a cohesive look. Tonally matching all materials ties together flooring, seat, panel and weather equipment areas within your interior. By matching the carpet binding colour to the seats and panels, the eye is drawn seamlessly across the interior. These schemes don't look for exact colour matching, but celebrate the highs and lows of shading across all materials.

Contrast

Contrasting schemes use colour to accent between different areas of the interior. In this scheme, the primary colour of the seats and panels is made to stand out against the secondary contrast colour of the carpets and flooring. Keep carpet edging tonally matching to the carpet colour to clearly define between the seating and floor areas — creating a striking interior with real visual impact.

Connect

These schemes use colour to connect different areas of the car for a unified finish. You may want to tie in the bodywork paint colour to your seat piping, or perhaps match the carpet edging to the colour of your seats. Connect schemes often incorporate the exterior colour of the car — using piping and binding as an opportunity to bridge the outside and inside worlds of your restoration.

Three Helpful Tips

  • Regardless of the colour scheme — Complement, Contrast or Connect — in all three cases, the seats and panels are always the same colour as each other.
  • Restorations that combine a maximum of three colours (including bodywork) are often the most successful in appearance — striking a balance of colour interest without being over-complicated.
  • Panel and seat work is the most prominent colour seen inside your car — referred to as the 'Primary' material. Choose this first, then build your 'Secondary' materials (carpet, moquette, hardura) around it.
