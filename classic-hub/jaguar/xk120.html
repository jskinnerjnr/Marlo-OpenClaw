Overview

The XK120 is the car that saved Jaguar. Launched at the 1948 Earls Court Motor Show as what was intended to be a styling exercise to showcase the new XK twin-cam engine, demand was so overwhelming that William Lyons was forced to put it into production. The name referred to its claimed 120mph top speed — verified in 1949 when a modified XK120 set a new world production car speed record of 132.6mph at Jabbeke, Belgium. It was the first Jaguar with independent front suspension (torsion bars) and rack-and-pinion steering.

ⓘ The XK engine was designed during World War Two by William Heynes, Walter Hassan and Claude Baily — originally conceived during wartime fire-watching shifts. It went on to power Jaguars until 1992, and is considered one of the finest engines of the twentieth century.

Racing heritage

The XK120 formed the basis of the competition C-Type, which won Le Mans in 1951 and 1953. Works-prepared XK120s competed at Le Mans and in international rallying before the C-Type took over as the dedicated competition car.

Aluminium-bodied OTS cars are among the most valuable XK120s. Verify body material before purchase — an alloy-bodied car commands a dramatically higher premium (~3–5×) over a comparable steel car.

Factory body colours

Jaguar paint codes are the manufacturer's internal codes from the relevant era. Hex values are digital approximations only — actual colours vary between batches, age, and paint supplier. Always verify against a Heritage Certificate (available from the British Motor Industry Heritage Trust at heritage.org.uk) or an original factory chip before use in restoration.

The early aluminium-bodied cars (1948–early 1950) had a very limited colour palette — primarily Cream/Ivory. The steel-body cars from 1950 onwards offered a broader range including polychromatic (metallic) finishes from approximately 1951.
