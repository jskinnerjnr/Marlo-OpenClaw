Overview

The XJ-S replaced the E-Type in 1975 — but it was a different kind of car entirely. Where the E-Type was a sports car, the XJ-S was a grand tourer: quieter, more refined, better suited to long motorway miles than a mountain road. Initial design work was by the aerodynamicist Malcolm Sayer (who died in 1970 before the car reached production), completed by the in-house team. The flying buttresses — polarising at launch, now iconic — were an aerodynamic feature, not a styling gimmick. Despite a disastrous launch into the 1973 oil crisis and early unreliability, the XJ-S became one of the longest-lived Jaguar models and is now highly regarded.

⚠ Avoid pre-HE cars (pre-1981) unless very well sorted. The H.E. (High Efficiency) V12 from 1981 is the sweet spot — dramatically improved economy and reliability. Pre-HE cars have complex fuel systems and poor economy.

The flying buttresses — facts, not fiction

The flying buttresses are often wrongly described as a vestige of a mid-engine layout that was never built. They are an aerodynamic feature inspired by Malcolm Sayer's research — part of the rear downforce management on what was designed to be a high-speed GT. They did cause a practical problem: restricted rearward vision meant each car initially required individual type approval in Germany. This was eventually resolved, but the XJ-S initially sold poorly in Germany as a result.

Factory body colours

Jaguar paint codes are the manufacturer's internal codes from the relevant era. Hex values are digital approximations only — actual colours vary between batches, age, and paint supplier. Always verify against a Heritage Certificate (available from the British Motor Industry Heritage Trust at heritage.org.uk) or an original factory chip before use in restoration.

The XJ-S/XJS was produced for 21 years across three distinct eras with substantially different colour palettes. Colours listed below represent the known range; individual model years varied. Specific paint codes for the XJ-S era require verification from Heritage Certificate records (BMIHT) or specialist club archives (JEC).
