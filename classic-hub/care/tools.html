Workshop Essentials

Tools & Fixings

Interior restoration requires a specific set of tools — some familiar, some surprisingly specialist. This guide covers everything from the adhesives that actually hold trim in place to the fixing clips that locate a door card, plus the tools that make the job possible without damaging 50-year-old materials.

1. Adhesives

Getting adhesive choice right is probably the single most important decision in trim work. Too weak and panels fall off; too aggressive and you can't reposition; wrong type and it bleeds through or degrades the material. These are the adhesives that professional trimmers actually use.

Dunlop S758 Contact Adhesive ★ Marlo Recommends
Dunlop

The industry standard for classic car trim — used by professional trimmers including John Skinner (Manufacturing) Ltd. A solvent-based contact adhesive that bonds leather, leathercloth, carpet, headlining and foam to metal, wood and hardboard. Apply to both surfaces, allow to tack off (2–5 minutes), then press together for an immediate permanent bond. Repositioning is not possible once contact is made — measure twice.

Available in: 1L tin, 2.5L tin, 5L tin. Brush or spreader application.

2. Interior Fixings & Clips

Classic British cars used an array of proprietary clips, spire nuts and trim pins that are often missing or broken by the time a car reaches restoration. Getting the right fixing in the right place matters — the wrong clip either doesn't hold or damages the panel behind it.

3. Wadding, Foam & Underlay

The materials between the outer trim and the bodywork are as important as the trim itself — they determine whether seats are comfortable, whether door cards rattle, and how the finished job looks and feels. Don't scrimp here.

Rubberised Hair (Hessian-Backed) ★ Marlo Recommends
Various

Traditional seat and back cushion filling for pre-1970 cars. Rubberised curled horse hair gives the correct period feel and profile — foam substitutes look and feel different, and compress differently over time. Used in TR seat squabs, E-Type buckets, and Healey bench elements. Also available from specialist upholstery suppliers.

Buy on Amazon →
High-Density Foam Sheet (25mm / 50mm)
Various

For seat cushion replacement on later cars (post-1970) and for backing door card padding. Use medium to high-density foam (35–45kg/m³) for seats — soft foam compresses too quickly. Sheets can be cut with a serrated bread knife or electric carving knife.

Jute Felt / Carpet Underlay
Various

The traditional underlayer beneath classic car carpets — jute felt gives the correct feel and some insulation. Modern felt-backed carpet sometimes dispenses with a separate underlay, but fitting a 5mm jute layer significantly improves comfort and finish.

Self-Adhesive Foam Tape (10mm / 3mm)
Various

Essential for sealing door cards against rattles — a 3mm strip of self-adhesive foam around the perimeter of a door card eliminates the buzzing that comes from panel-to-door contact. Also used for sealing hood frame joints.

Sound Deadening Mat (Bitumen or Butyl)
Various

Applied to floor pans, door skins and transmission tunnel before fitting carpet. Reduces road and transmission noise significantly. Butyl-based products (e.g. STP S10, Dynamat) are odour-free and don't melt in summer heat. Bitumen mats are cheaper but can smell when hot.

4. Hand Tools

Interior work doesn't demand a large tool collection — but the right tools save a great deal of time and prevent damage to irreplaceable materials.

Trim Removal Tool Set ★ Marlo Recommends
Various

Plastic and nylon pry tools for removing door cards, kick panels and trim pieces without scratching paint or cracking aged plastic clips. A basic 5-piece set covers most classic car trim removal work. Never use a screwdriver to lever trim — you will crack the panel or the paint.

Awl / Bradawl
Various

For piercing leather and leathercloth to locate fixing holes, and for starting pilot holes in hardboard backing. A sharp awl makes clean holes — a blunt one tears material. Keep it sharp.

Heat Gun (Variable Temperature)
Various

Two uses in trim work: warming leather and leathercloth to make them pliable around curves and corners; and softening old adhesive to aid removal. A variable temperature gun (300°C–600°C) covers both. Don't use a hair dryer — not hot enough. Don't use a blowtorch — too hot.

For rewrapping wiring looms during interior removal and refitting. Period-correct loom tape is fabric-backed — self-amalgamating tape is useful for waterproofing joints. Essential when disturbing any wiring.

5. Lifting & Safety

Before any underfloor work — fitting sound deadening, checking sill condition, or accessing transmission tunnel fixings — the car needs to be safely raised and supported.

Trolley Jack (2-tonne minimum)
Various

A low-profile trolley jack is essential for classic cars — many have minimal ground clearance and a standard floor jack won't reach the jacking points. Look for a profile under 85mm at minimum height. Aluminium racing jacks are lighter; steel jacks are cheaper.

Buy on Amazon →
Axle Stands (pair, 2-tonne rated) ★ Marlo Recommends
Various

NEVER work under a car supported only by a jack. Axle stands are not optional. A minimum of two stands under the car at all times when working underneath — four stands is better for full underfloor access. Rated to 2 tonnes per stand minimum.

Rubber Jacking Pads
Various

Classic car sills, jacking points and floor pans are frequently thin or corroded. Rubber jacking pads distribute load and prevent the jack from punching through. Essential for Spitfire, Midget and MGB sills especially.

When the job needs a professional

Some work is better left to specialists

Cutting and sewing leather to original patterns, matching period-correct materials, and fitting complex hoods and soft tops correctly is a specialist skill. If the interior of your car needs more than minor refreshing, a professional trim kit from a specialist manufacturer — cut to the original factory patterns — will give a better result than hand-cutting from bulk material.
