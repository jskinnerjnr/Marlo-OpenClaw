Overview — the Spridget story

The Austin-Healey Sprite and MG Midget share a single platform and are collectively known among enthusiasts as the "Spridget." Both were designed by Donald Healey's team (styling by Gerry Coker and Geoffrey Healey) and built at BMC's Abingdon factory. The MG Midget was launched simultaneously with the Mk2 Sprite in 1961 — sharing identical mechanicals but wearing MG badging, a different grille, and slightly better trim at a small premium. The two models evolved in lockstep until the Sprite was discontinued in 1971; the Midget continued until 1979 with the controversial Triumph-engined 1500 variant.

ⓘ The Mk1 Frogeye/Bugeye is one of the most characterful British sports cars ever made. The headlight retraction mechanism was cut from the design to save cost — the headlights simply sit on top of the bonnet, giving the car its distinctive frog-eyed look.

MG Midget 1500 (1974–1979)

The Midget 1500 is a separate chapter — politically contentious within British Leyland, it used a Triumph Spitfire engine rather than the traditional A-Series.
