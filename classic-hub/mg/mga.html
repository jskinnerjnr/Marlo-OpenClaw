Overview

The MGA was MG's first genuinely modern sports car — abandoning the pre-war separate-chassis styling of the TF for a sleek envelope body designed by Syd Enever, named by general manager John Thornley. Announced at the Frankfurt Motor Show in 1955, it was immediately popular and ran to over 101,000 units across seven years. Only 5,869 were sold in the UK — the rest went to export markets, predominantly the USA. Body-on-frame construction with independent front suspension and rack-and-pinion steering.

ⓘ The Twin Cam is the rarest MGA and the most spectacular on paper — but its reputation for unreliability made it a commercial failure. Modern engine building techniques can address its problems, but it remains a car for specialists.

Factory Body Colours

The MGA used BMC paint codes throughout its production (1955–1962). BMC codes of this era were typically letter-number combinations (e.g. BU for blue, GN for green). Specific codes have not been confirmed from primary sources accessed during research — the table below lists all documented colours with notes on availability. Hex swatches are digital approximations only. Verify via a BMIHT Heritage Certificate before any restoration work.

ⓘ Twin Cam colour range: The MGA Twin Cam (1958–1960) was not offered in a restricted colour palette compared to the standard car — it was available in the same range as the 1500 of its period. However, the vast majority of surviving Twin Cams appear in white, BRG and red, reflecting the preferences of buyers ordering the performance variant. There is no documented evidence of a factory-restricted Twin Cam colour list.

Interior Trim Colours

MGA interiors used leathercloth (Vynide) as standard. Genuine leather was available as a factory option on some models. Carpets were loop-pile throughout production.
