Why Interior Trim Deserves Your Full Attention

The most subjective element of any restoration. The part you see every day. The part most often treated as an afterthought.

8 min read

Open the bonnet of a concours-winning Triumph TR6 and a well-restored but unexceptional one. Look at the engines. You will struggle to tell them apart. The components are the same. The materials are specified. The correct rebuild produces a correct engine.

Now sit in both cars. The difference is immediate and unambiguous. The quality of the leather, the precision of the pattern cutting, the alignment of the piping, the fit of the carpet around the transmission tunnel — these things tell the story of the restoration far more honestly than anything under the bonnet.

The standardisation problem

Interior trim has no equivalent of the parts catalogue. There is no single correct specification for a Triumph TR6 seat cover in the way there is a correct part number for a TR6 wheel cylinder.

What exists instead is a combination of factory records (often incomplete), original surviving examples (each slightly different), Heritage Certificate data (the most reliable primary source), and the accumulated knowledge of the trimming specialists who have worked on these cars for decades.

This means that the quality of a trim kit depends not just on the material and the price, but on the knowledge of the manufacturer. Do they know the correct grain direction for the seat facings? Do they understand the original piping diameter? Are their patterns derived from factory originals or from a previous supplier's kit that was itself derived from another kit of unknown origin?

The answer varies enormously. And you cannot tell from a photograph.

What you live with

Consider how you actually experience your car.

The engine — once it starts without drama — recedes into background noise. The suspension becomes the feel of the road. The brakes are confidence, not something you consciously notice. All of these elements matter enormously, but once they're right, they become invisible.

The interior is different. Every time you open the door, you see it. Every time you sit down, you feel the leather or leathercloth under your hands. Every time a passenger climbs in, the first thing they see is the quality of the trim. At a show, it's what people lean in to look at. It's what the judge examines closely.

The interior is the part of the car you have a daily relationship with. It should reflect that.

What separates a good kit from a poor one

Five things distinguish a properly made trim kit from a budget alternative:

1. Pattern accuracy. Original patterns derived from factory records or original cars produce the correct shape — the way the leather folds over the seat bolster, the way the door card follows the inner panel contour. Patterns derived from previous kits accumulate errors. A good manufacturer knows the difference.

2. Material. Period-correct Connolly hide, Ambla leathercloth, wool loop carpet — these feel and behave differently from modern substitutes. The difference is tangible the moment you sit in the car.

3. Cutting. Leather cut on the cross grain to save material distorts when fitted. Carpet cut slightly out of square never lies flat. Precision cutting is a skilled operation, not a production shortcut.

4. Fitment. A trim kit is only as good as its installation. A professionally made kit should be designed to fit without modification. If it needs cutting and stretching to fit, the pattern is wrong.

5. Finishing. Piping, binding, edge finishing — the details that separate a professional result from an amateur one. These take time and skill. They are the first thing a knowledgeable person notices.

The turning heads argument

There is a practical commercial argument here as well, for anyone who intends to sell their restoration.

Cars with exceptional interiors sell for more. Not a little more — significantly more. A buyer who opens the door of a car and sees a perfectly fitted, correctly specified, properly made interior immediately understands that they are looking at a car where no corner was cut. It changes the entire negotiation.

Conversely, a car with mechanical work that cost ten times the interior budget, but a mediocre interior, will always struggle to realise its potential value. The interior is visible. The engine rebuild is not.
