What to Look For When Buying a Classic

A crash course for first-time buyers on what matters, what doesn't, and what the seller probably won't tell you.

9 min read

The moment you fall in love with a car is the moment your judgement becomes least reliable. This is the central problem of buying a classic. The car you're looking at has been living in someone else's garage, possibly for decades, possibly not very well maintained, possibly with creative history — and you need to evaluate it clearly at precisely the point when you most want to say yes.

This guide won't prevent you from buying the wrong car. Nothing will. But it might slow you down long enough to buy a less wrong one.

The four condition grades

Classic car ads use condition descriptions loosely. Here's what they should mean:

Concours. Restored to the highest standard, correct in all details, show-winning quality. These cars are rare, expensive, and often not the best drivers — concours restorations prioritise appearance over use.

Good. Well-presented, mechanically sound, no significant rust or structural issues. May have had professional restoration work. Drives well and can be used regularly without apology.

Driver. Roadworthy and usable but showing its age. May have cosmetic issues, older paint, some minor rust. Good honest classics fall here — not beautiful, but honest and driveable.

Project. Requires significant work to be roadworthy. May be off the road, incomplete, or structurally compromised. Projects are where the value lies for people with skills, time and patience — and where inexperienced buyers most often come to grief.

In practice, most ads overstate condition by at least one grade. "Good" often means "Driver." "Driver" often means "Project." Read accordingly.

The three things that kill a budget

Hidden rust. Structural rust — floor pans, sills, chassis outriggers, inner wings — is expensive and time-consuming to repair properly. Surface rust is manageable; structural rust is a different project entirely. Bring a torch and a screwdriver. Poke things. Look underneath. The seller who won't let you look underneath is telling you something.

Engine history. An engine that's been rebuilt properly is worth paying for. An engine of unknown history that "runs fine" may run fine for another thousand miles or need a full rebuild next month. Ask for evidence of work done. Receipts. Invoices. Names of mechanics. An engine with no history should be priced accordingly.

Missing parts. Classic cars frequently get parted out over their lives. Chrome trim that's "in the boot somewhere." Dashboard instruments that "need calibrating." A hood that "just needs the frame." Missing parts for rare British classics can be expensive, scarce or both. Get a complete car. The £500 saving on an incomplete car often costs £3,000 to fix.

The Heritage Certificate

For many British classics — Triumph, Jaguar, MG, Austin-Healey — a Heritage Certificate is available from the relevant manufacturer's archive. It documents what the car left the factory as: engine number, body number, original colour, original trim specification.

This matters for two reasons. First, it's the most reliable way to verify the car's identity — particularly important for higher-value examples where fraud, although uncommon, exists. Second, it tells you what the car should look like, which matters enormously if you're planning a restoration.

Heritage Certificates typically cost £40–80 and take a few weeks. Ask the seller if they have one. If they don't, get one yourself after purchase. The British Motor Museum Heritage Trust issues certificates for most volume British marques.

The pre-purchase inspection

Hire a specialist, not a general mechanic. This is the single most important practical advice in this guide. A general mechanic may be an excellent mechanic — but they will not know what a Triumph TR6 sill should look like, what a Jaguar E-Type's floor pan typically does, or where an Austin-Healey 3000 hides its worst rust. A marque specialist will. The relevant owners' clubs can recommend inspectors.

A pre-purchase inspection costs £100–200. Against the price of a classic car and the cost of a restoration, this is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

Red flags sellers hope you won't notice

Fresh underseal. Applied recently to a car that's been standing. Underseal hides rust. Fresh underseal over old rust is a common trick.

Damp carpets. Lift the carpet. Feel the floor. Wet floors mean leaking windscreen seals, leaking door seals, or corroded floor pans. Any of these is a significant job.

Mismatched panel gaps. Uneven panel gaps that suggest the car has been in an accident and repaired. Not always a problem if the repair was done properly, but worth investigating.

Over-fresh paint. A car that's been resprayed recently — particularly if the engine bay and door shuts don't match — may have had bodywork done to hide problems rather than fix them properly.

Numbers that don't match. On higher-value cars especially — E-Types, Healey 3000s, early TRs — check that the engine number and chassis number match the logbook and (if available) the Heritage Certificate.

Where to buy

Club classifieds. The best source for honest cars. Club members know the marque, know what they have, and have reputations to protect. The TSSC, MGOC, Healey Club and Jaguar Drivers' Club all run classified sections. Start here.

Specialist dealers. More expensive, but offer some protection. A reputable specialist dealer will not knowingly sell a rotten car — their business depends on repeat custom and recommendation. Ask around the clubs for recommended dealers.

Auctions. Buyer beware in its purest form. You may view briefly, you buy as seen, and you pay a buyer's premium. Auctions are where specialists buy — and where bargains exist for people who know exactly what they're looking at. For a first-time buyer, auctions are not recommended.

General classifieds (eBay, AutoTrader, Facebook Marketplace). Fine as a starting point for finding examples, but apply maximum scepticism. The cars with the most enthusiastic descriptions and the best photographs are not always the best cars.
