The One-Stop-Shop Trap

Buying everything from one supplier feels convenient. Here's why it often isn't.

6 min read

There's an understandable logic to the one-stop-shop. You're restoring a classic — a project that already demands time, patience and money in quantities you probably underestimated. The appeal of a single supplier who can provide the steering wheel, the gearbox rebuild kit, the body panels and the interior trim is obvious. One relationship. One invoice. One delivery.

The problem is that convenience and quality pull in opposite directions. A supplier who covers everything from mechanical parts to interior trim to chrome is, by definition, a generalist. And in classic car restoration, the devil is almost entirely in the detail.

Where it works

To be fair, the one-stop-shop model holds up reasonably well for mechanical and running gear components. A wheel cylinder is a wheel cylinder. The tolerances are specified, the material requirements are defined, and a correctly manufactured part from a generalist supplier will perform as well as one from a specialist. The same is broadly true for brake lines, gaskets, fasteners, and most electrical components. These are standardised products with measurable performance criteria.

Where it falls apart

Interior trim is where the one-stop model breaks down — and it breaks down badly.

The reason is straightforward: interior trim is not a standardised product. Unlike a brake cylinder, a seat cover or a carpet set has no single correct specification. It has material requirements, pattern requirements, cutting requirements, and fitting requirements — and the quality of the end result depends entirely on how seriously each of those is taken.

A generalist supplier who also sells axle oil and wiper blades is unlikely to employ a master trimmer. They are more likely to source trim kits from a contract manufacturer working to a price point, using whatever material comes closest to the original at the required margin. The kit will be described as 'correct for the model.' It may even look acceptable in a photograph.

In the car, on your seats, with afternoon light through the windows — the difference between a properly made trim kit and a budget alternative is immediately apparent to anyone who has ever sat in an original car.

The material question

Original Connolly hide — the leather used in Jaguar, Bentley and many British sports cars from the 1920s onwards — has a specific hand, grain and weight. Period-correct Ambla leathercloth, used on MGB, Spitfire and Austin-Healey interiors, has a particular texture and backing that distinguishes it from modern PVC substitutes. Wool loop carpet behaves differently from cut-pile, and differently again from the synthetic alternatives that dominate the budget end of the market.

A specialist interior trim manufacturer works with these materials because they understand what period-correct means and why it matters. A generalist sources what's available at the right price.

The recommendation

The practical takeaway is simple: use generalist suppliers for the standardised items — mechanical parts, fasteners, seals — where quality is consistent and specification is measurable. For interior trim, find a specialist. The difference in cost is smaller than most people expect. The difference in result is much larger.
