Overview

The Triumph Stag is one of the most characterful British GTs of the 1970s — a 2+2 convertible with a distinctive T-bar roll hoop (both structural and compliant with anticipated US rollover regulations), styled by Michelotti and powered by a purpose-built 2997cc V8. MacPherson strut front suspension, semi-trailing arm rear, power rack-and-pinion steering and a choice of automatic or 4-speed manual transmission. A removable hardtop was a popular option. With the right history, it is a genuinely beautiful and enjoyable car.

⚠ The Stag's reputation for unreliability is real — but manageable. A well-maintained, correctly-coolanted Triumph V8 Stag is a pleasure to own. A neglected one will destroy itself. Read the reliability section carefully before buying.
The Reliability Issue — explained

The Triumph V8's reputation was damaged by three specific engineering issues, all of which are now well understood:

IssueCauseConsequence
1. Water pump positionThe pump is positioned unusually high in the cooling circuit — a legacy of shared machining tooling with the Saab 99 slant-four. If coolant drops below the pump level, circulation stops.No circulation = rapid overheating. Happens before the temperature gauge registers the problem.
2. Iron block, alloy headsDissimilar metals in the cooling system cause electrolytic corrosion without year-round inhibitor. White sludge (aluminium oxide) blocks the radiator and galleries silently.Blocked cooling, head gasket failure, head damage. The "white death" of neglected Stags.
3. Timing chainLong simplex roller timing chains were prone to failure under 25,000 miles on early cars. A stretched or failed chain causes catastrophic engine damage.Many engines were destroyed. Listen for rattle on cold start — the first warning sign.
ⓘ The solution: Year-round antifreeze/inhibitor at correct concentration, regular coolant changes, timing chain replacement at recommended intervals, and careful temperature monitoring. Owners who follow this regime have trouble-free engines. Many Rover V8 swaps exist — a V8 Stag is more reliable but less original and commands a lower value.
